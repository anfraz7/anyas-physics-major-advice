This page is specifically about prestigious international fellowships like the Rhodes, Marshall, Churchill, Fulbright, etc. If you're interested in applying to a fellowship like these, the UNC Office of Distinguished Scholarships (ODS) will be your best friend. The application process is intense and really really competitive. That being said, you definitely won’t get it if you don’t apply, and I found the application process an extremely valuable experience in itself. 

### Applying is not always worth it

These are highly competitive and time-intensive and often put a lot more emphasis on grades than PhD applications do. Most successful applicants will have a 4.0. I don’t want to discourage anyone from applying---I was discouraged from applying but am glad I chose to anyways. But, these are time intensive applications that can take away from time you might be putting into more realistic applications like the GRFP or grad schools, so pick your bets wisely.

However, I will make a little note that the Fulbright is a bit less competitive than the others, and they typically look for a diverse range of interests and life experiences in their applicants to my understanding. Worth looking into if you think it might be for you!

No matter what, you must schedule an appointment with ODS during your junior year to talk about these programs.

I applied to the Churchill Scholarship and was ultimately unsuccessful. However, it was a very intense process and I learned a LOT, so I’ve included a lil blog post reflecting on it below. Hopefully by sharing this I can demonstrate that there are some pretty cool international connections out there just waiting to be made.


### My experience applying to the Churchill Scholarship

The Churchill Scholarship funds a small cohort of US students to pursue a one year research-based masters degree (MPhil) at the University of Cambridge in the UK. The program covers tuition, fees, and airfare, and provides a living stipend for the year. The MPhil is not typically a funded degree, so this is one of the few options available to fund it.

I had been wanting to apply for one of these prestigious international fellowships ever since the summer after my freshman year. My REU roommate that summer was applying for a Fulbright, and she was in contact with several professors at universities abroad who were interested in hosting her. I thought that was so cool! I ultimately chose the Churchill because I liked the fact that they sent a 10-20 person cohort to a single school, rather than to multiple schools, and because it was specifically for science students while others tend to favor applicants in the humanities.

I scheduled a meeting with ODS at the end of my junior year, and they gave me the low-down on the UNC endorsement process and answered my application questions. UNC can only endorse two students per year for the scholarship, and you must submit a completed Churchill Scholarship application to UNC by the endorsement deadline.

In addition to typical application questions, the application requires a statement of purpose, a research proposal, and an email from a professor at Cambridge saying they would be willing to host you in their lab for a year if your application is successful.

Over the summer, I reached out to a professor at Cambridge that I was interested in working with to introduce myself and ask if he was taking MPhil students next year. He was very kind and responded affirmatively. I told him about the Churchill Scholarship and the proposal it required, and he asked me to write my proposal and then send it to him to read over.

While doing some reading for my proposal over the summer, I also reached out to two former Churchill scholars, who were happy to set up Zoom meetings with me. One of these students had completed her MPhil in with the professor that I was interested in, and she connected me with the second student who had completed the MPhil track that I was interested in. These meetings were pretty helpful. The second student even emailed the leaders of the Churchill Foundation after I pointed out a discrepancy relevant to the MPhil track I was interested in on their website, which helped me clear up that question.

I spent the two weeks after I got back from an REU that summer in the library seven hours a day reading papers. The research I was proposing was in a different sub-field than my current research, so I had to first orient myself in that field and then find an interesting research question based on the research done in this group at Cambridge. I ended up choosing a paper written by a previous MPhil student from the group to base my proposal off of, proposing to use a model that she had created to further study her material system of interest. Once I had a one page proposal written I sent it to a previous research mentor from UNC to look over, and he gave me lots of excellent feedback. 

Finally, I sent my proposal to the professor at Cambridge. That was TERRIFYING. But, he was so kind! He started by validating my research question as an interesting one (thank goodness), and then pointed out that the methods I suggested wouldn’t really work. He suggested a different method and sent me a paper from his group to read on this new method. I modified my proposal and sent it to the professor a second time, and he gave me the thumbs up. He also sent me the email I needed for my application, which said that he would be willing to host me in his research group if my application was successful.

As I was finishing up my proposal, I was also working on my statement of purpose. Being my first application of the season, this also involved several drafts. ODS is happy to help with Churchill essays, so I met with them twice to talk about both my statement of purpose and my proposal. They had very helpful comments on both.

The Churchill Scholarship requires four letters of recommendation (eek!). I reached out to all four of my recommenders over the summer to confirm that they would write letters for me. The Churchill Scholarship took a bit of explaining, and I met with two of my recommenders in September to discuss the research I was proposing. I made sure to send them reminder emails in the days leading up to the endorsement deadline, and I sent them all copies of my statement of purpose, proposal, and CV.

I submitted my proposal the morning it was due, then received an email from one of my recommenders requesting to meet to make some suggestions on my proposal which were all very helpful, then implemented those recommendations, emailed ODS, and resubmitted (lol). 

I heard back from ODS a few weeks later letting me know that my application had been unsuccessful, and I was not chosen as one of the endorsed UNC students. I was of course disappointed, but also knew that this was the most likely outcome—Churchill Scholars have some of the most intimidating LinkedIn pages on the planet. But that’s okay! I went in knowing that, and I’m ultimately glad I applied. I got two big benefits out of applying: 

Firstly, I gained a lot of confidence in my research abilities and paper-reading skills. I read dozens of papers then wrote a research proposal for a field I had zero experience in, then sent that proposal to a professor at one of the most prestigious universities in the world. And he didn’t tell me I was stupid!! When it came time to write my GRFP proposal, it certainly wasn't a piece of cake but it was a lot easier to write a proposal within my current research field after having already written this Churchill proposal in a new field. 

Secondly, I identified a research area that I ended up choosing to pursue my PhD in. When reading the papers from professors in these groups, I was able to grasp onto the language they were using a lot more easily having done this literature review for my Churchill proposal. My new PI is actually a frequent collaborator of the Cambridge professor I applied for, so I feel as though I've already made some connections in my new research field.

Well, I also got a third benefit from applying for the Churchill: I chose to apply to the MPhil at Cambridge anyways after I was rejected from the Churchill, and ended up being admitted without even doing an interview. The MPhil application doesn’t even require a research proposal, so I’m pretty sure the fact that I was able to include one in my application was a big help. I also applied for the Gates Cambridge Fellowship, which is the only other fellowship which will fully fund MPhil students in the field I applied for. Unfortunately, I was also unsuccessful in this application and will not attend Cambridge next year--—but hey, I got in!

Although I learned a lot, there were also some big cons to applying that I didn’t anticipate. For one, I didn’t take any breaks this summer: I spent the weeks before going to my REU finishing up work in my previous lab, and then I spent the weeks after the REU in the library writing my Churchill proposal. Because of that, I went into my senior fall already burnt out. This made for a pretty miserable fall semester. To be honest, I’m not certain that taking those two weeks after the REU to rest would have completely prevented this, but they probably would have helped. Research, applications, and classes that semester felt like pulling teeth. For another, I chose to focus on the Churchill Scholarship at the expense of some of my other applications, particularly the GRFP. I think the path I chose gave me the most personal growth as a researcher, but I do wonder how using that time and zest to work on my GRFP materials would have changed my professional outcomes. Ooooh, that is a dangerous game to play though...

If I had to do it again, I would have applied for a DPhil (PhD) at Cambridge simultaneously to the MPhil. Doctoral programs at Cambridge are more likely to be fully funded than MPhils, and to my understanding the Gates Cambridge scholarship also prefers them. The DPhil requires a research proposal, but I already had one from my Churchill application so it wouldn’t have been too hard to add on there. Oh well—hindsight is 20/20! A note to anyone thinking of applying though :)

Overall though, I really did get a lot out of the application process. I also found example essays [here](https://naveenraman.com/writing/fellowships.html) which were useful. Applying for a fellowship like this is a lot of work, but if you’re considering it then I highly encourage you to schedule an appointment with ODS and to apply if it’s something you really want. They’re competitive, but you’ll never get it if you don’t apply. Plus, you might end up gaining something from the application process that you didn’t expect, even if you don’t win.
