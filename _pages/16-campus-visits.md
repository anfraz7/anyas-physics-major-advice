Campus visits are so cool! I had so much fun getting to fly all over the country for my visits. Make a vacation of it!! I literally spent a day exploring the Golden Gate Bridge on a university-sponsored trip.

A campus visit is when a school pays for you to travel to their campus for a tour of their department. They come in two different flavors: admitted student visits, and in-person interviews. My experience was that the two physics programs that I got into had admitted student visits, and the two materials science programs that I was admitted into had in-person interviews.

Even if you are there for an interview, your campus visit is the chance to decide if you can see yourself working in that department and living in that part of the country for the next 4-6 years. Take advantage of it!


### my two cents

- Pack business casual clothes. Business casual is imperative if it’s an in-person interview, but my experience with admitted student visits also had lots of people dressed to impress.
- Pack a small notepad for advisor visits. I literally had so many advisor visits that I walked into and wished I had brought one.
- Email professors you are interested in BEFORE the campus visit, particularly if it is an in-person interview.
- Don’t try to work on the plane. Just my opinion. I travelled way more than I ever have in my life this past semester, and it’s EXHAUSTING. Get as much schoolwork done as you can in advance, and work with your professors to get extensions for what you can’t. It’s important to feel relaxed and clearheaded during these visits, instead of headache-y and preoccupied.


### in-person interviews

My personal and secondhand experience with in-person interviews is that if you are invited to interview at a school, that means that you will only be admitted if a professor at the university recommends you for admission. Therefore, your goal for the visit is to make good connections with several professors. It’s super important to email professors you are interested in well in advance of the visit to get your name on their radar and make sure they save you an interview slot. Immediately after the interviews, follow up with all the professors you are still interested in to express gratitude and continued interest.

I know of several professors at UC Berkeley (materials science), including one I interviewed with, that asked students to commit via email the same day of the interview if the professor was our top choice. In my case they just meant top choice at UCB, but in other people’s cases they meant top choice of all schools…..as in, if that professor admitted them then they would commit to UCB immediately. Honestly UCB is the only school I’ve heard of that happening, but I guess it’s an extreme case to illustrate why you should have a clear idea of what research groups you want to join going into an in-person interview.
