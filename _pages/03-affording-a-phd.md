**Most PhD programs are paid jobs!**

Unlike your undergraduate degree, you shouldn’t have to pay for a PhD in physics or any related field. You won’t be making as much as you would in industry, but most schools cover tuition and pay a living stipend.

Graduate students are paid in one of three ways: Teaching Assistantships (TAs), Research Assistantships (RAs), and fellowships. A typical physics PhD student will TA for their first year or two, then hopefully transition to RA for the remainder of their degree. My experience applying to materials science PhDs has been different—all of the materials science programs I got into offered me (and everyone who got in) an RA position right off the bat.

Winning a fellowship is separate from getting into a school, and often requires a separate application. There are big fellowships that many students apply to: in my case, I applied to the NSF GRFP and the DOE CSGF. There are also individual school fellowships that you will be considered for automatically. The biggest benefit of a fellowship is intellectual freedom: RA money comes from specific project grants that you or your research advisor have written and won, while fellowships can support whatever project you would like to pursue. Being supported by a fellowship gives you more freedom when picking a thesis topic. Additionally, winning an external fellowship like the GRFP or CSGF can allow you to appeal admission decisions at schools that rejected you.

**Many application fees can be waived!** 

Don’t let application fees be a barrier to grad school until you’ve done some extensive looking into all your options. The vast majority of schools have a way you can get the fee waived. However, it’s important to start this process EARLY because the closer to the due date you are, the harder it will be to find a fee waiver. (See the “Fee Waivers” tab for more info.)
